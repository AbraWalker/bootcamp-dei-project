There are a lot of terms used by the LGBT+ community to identify people, and sometimes they can get pretty confusing!

Sexuality

Refers to a man who is attracted to men. Some non-binary people also identify with this term. Gay is also a generic term for lesbian and gay sexuality. For example, some women define themselves as gay rather than lesbian.

Refers to a woman who is attracted to women. Some non-binary people also indentify with this term.

Refers to someone who is attracted to more than one gender. Bisexual people may describe themselves with a variety of terms, including bisexual, pansexual, or simply queer.

Refers to a person whose attraction towards others doesn’t regard sex or gender. Usually this term falls under the 'bisexual' umbrella, though some people believe it is important to make a distinction between the two terms.

A person who does not experience sexual attraction. Some asexual people experience romantic attraction, while others don't. These people may also refer to themselves with other sexuality terms, like gay or lesbian, to describe their romantic attractions.

Prejudice or negative attitudes, beliefs or views about gay people. This can include the fear or dislike of someone because they are, or are perceived to be, gay.

Gender

A person's innate sense of ebing a man, woman, non-binary, or another gender. Gender norms, roles and behaviours exist that are typically associated with being a woman, girl, man or boy. They are often expressed in terms of masculinity and femininity, and these roles and norms vary across cultures. Usually, a person's gender is assumed from the sex they were assigned at birth.

Someone whose gender matches the sex they were assigned at birth. For example, a cisgendered woman is someone who was born female and continues to live and identify as a woman.

A term to describe people whose gender is not usually associated with the sex they were assigned at birth. Transgender can also be used as an umbrella term for other gender descriptors, like non-binary, agender, transwoman, transman, and genderqueer. Depending on their expression, transgender people usually use "she/her" or "he/him" pronouns.

A term for people whose gender does not sit comfortably with common definitions of 'man' or 'woman'. Non-binary identities are varied and can include people who identify with some aspects of binary identity, while others reject them entirely. Non-binary people commonly use "they/them" pronouns.

The steps a transgender person will take to live in their gender. Each person's transition will involve different things. For some this involved medical intervention, like hormone therapy and surgery, however not all trans people want or are able to access this. Transitioning might also involve telling friends and family, using different pronouns, dressing differently and changing official documents.

Prejudice or negative attitudes, beliefs or views about trans people. This can include the fear or dislike of someone based on the fact they are, or are perceived to be, trans.

LGBT+ DEI in Schools

In April 2019, the government announced new regulations for teaching Relationships and Sex Education in England.

While there has been a lot of progress towards LGBT+ equality in recent decades, anti-LGBT bullying and language unfortunately remains commonplace in Britain's schools. Nearly half of all LGBT pupils still face bullying for who they are. In order to change this, Stonewall UK suggests that schools deliver a curriculum that encourages children and young people to respect those around them and appreciate difference.

The new regulations for teaching RSE include teaching about sexual orientation and gender identity "at a timely point". Schools should also be alive to issues such as everyday sexism, misogyny, homophobia and gender stereotypes, and take positive action to build a culture where these are not tolerated.

LGBT+ DEI in the Workplace

A CIPD study has shown that 40% of LGB+ workers and 55% of transgender workers have experienced workplace conflict and harrassment due to their sexuality or gender expression. Additionally, 16% of LGB+ workers and 18% of transgender workers feel psychologically unsafe, compared to 10% of straight workers who feel unsafe.

Studies like this show that workplaces still have a lot of work to do to provide a safe place to work for members of the LGBT+ community. First-steps to achieving this could include building awareness and taking action to implement trans-inclusive policies and practices.

Read more about the CIPD study here.

UK Employment Laws

- UK employment law, including the Equality Act 2010, protects LGBT+ people from workplace discrimination.
- The Equality Act 2010 prohibits direct and indirect discrimination, harrassment, and victimisation based on sexual orientation and - gender reassignment.
- Transgender individuals are protected by the Gender Recognition Act of 2004, safeguarding workplace rights.
- Employers should play a crucial role in promoting LGBT+ inclusion through diversity training and inclusive policies.
